Balance in Pokemon Games

Pokemon is one of the most popular video game franchises in the entire world, with over 370 million games sold worldwide, making it the second best-selling video game series in history. Pokemon has been around for over 24 years (first release was in February 1996), and throughout that time, nearly 900 Pokemon have been created.

Pokemon strength is measured by "stats", with higher stats generally equating to stronger Pokemon, and lower stats generally equating to weaker Pokemon. Pokemon can be grouped by many unique variables, including Generation (8 Generations with about 2-5 year gaps between generations), types (18 total types in Pokemon as of Generation 8), and status (Normal, Sub Legendary, Legendary, and Mythical Pokemon).

Pokemon is a very well balanced game in that most of these definining features are all well balanced against one another. In other words, there isn't usually a single kind of Pokemon that would dominate the others (the only exception being Pokemon status, but this is inherently balanced by the scarcity of the more specially classified statuses - more on this in the following sections). The analyses and visualizations below aim to demonstrate this conclusion.

Type Advantage on Specific Stats

When we look at individual stats, we can observe that there are some types which have higher than average stats in Attack or Special Attack and also in Defense or Special Defense. We explore this dynamic in the next section, which will show that these advantages in specific stats actually balance each other with disadvantages in other stats.

The rarity of these special Pokemon is the balancing component that limits these special Pokemon from dominating the game.

Stat Distributions by Generation

Pokemon are also classified by Generation - Each generation ranges from around 90 to 150 Pokemon, and represent about 2-5 year gap between previous generation releases. Determining if Pokemon are balanced across generations is a significant point of analysis, particularly because in video game series, game critics will point out how as time progresses, new characters (or Pokemon in this case) will sometimes steadily increase in power level which renders older characters/Pokemon obsolete (a phenomenon known as "powercreeping").

In [61]:
sb.violinplot(data=pokemon,x='generation',y='total_points',color=base_color,inner=None)
plt.ylabel('Stat Total')
plt.xlabel('Generation')
plt.title('Stat Total by Generation');

We can sort of see a small powercreep trend in Generations 1-4, noting that in Generation 4, there is a higher proportion of Pokemon resting in the higher peak range of stat totals as opposed to Generation 1 which was a more even bimodal distribution. However, you can immediately notice that this actually rebalanced in Generation 5, and then when a similar powercreep occurred from Generation 5 to Generations 6 and 7, Generation 8 rebalanced the distribution once more. These observations strongly support our balance claim by demonstrating that even in the presence of naturally occurring trends that push games to fall out of balance, Pokemon is actually quite diligent about recalibrating balance without letting something like powercreep go unchecked.

Special Pokemon Status by Generations

Now because we've established that "Special" Pokemon (Pokemons with the Sub Legendary, Legendary, or Mythical status) are inherently stronger than Normal Pokemon, we'd like to see if these Pokemon are fairly spread across the generations.

Note that our analysis thus far already suggests that this is likely the case, since the presence of too many of these Special Pokemon in one particular generation should've likely skewed the Stat vs Generation visualization we showed in the previous section towards the higher end.

g = sb.countplot(data = pokemon, x = 'generation', hue = 'special_status');

plt.xlabel('Generation')
plt.title('Special Pokemon by Generation')

For Generation and Pokemon Status, we can see that there were more special Pokemon added in the progression from Generation 1 to Generation 4, but then this stabilized going into Generations 5 and 6. Generation 7 showed a peak number of Sub Legendaries, but this was immediately remedied in Generation 8, which has the lowest number of Special Pokemon to date with just 3. This balancing trend is similar to the powercreeping balance we observed in the previous section.

Dragon types are the ones that have the slight edge - so Psychic's abundance of Special Pokemon ultimately did not cause a power imbalance in their favor.

Conclusion

As a franchise that has achieved numerous success over the past 24 years, Pokemon shows its experience in designing and maintaining strong balance amongst all of its hundreds of Pokemon, as demonstrated by its ability to balance Pokemon stats and strength across type, generation, and special status.
